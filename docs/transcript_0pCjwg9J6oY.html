Tim in San Diego, California writes in to ask this, "Pastor John, do you believe there's a difference between a Christian rap artist and a rap artist who is a Christian? And do you believe that an artist who is a Christian needs to include the gospel into every song that they make?" Well, let's start with that last question, because I think answering that one probably winds up addressing the concerns in the first one, which aren't as easy to answer, because the terms aren't clear. But this one is, "Does the gospel need to be in every song?" And if you mean the full enough doctrinal statement of the gospel that the listener would have a reasonable understanding of the gospel and know how to come to Christ, the answer is no. And I say that because Paul accused Peter in Galatians 2 of a kind of behavior that was not in step with the gospel. So there is the gospel, and there's life in step with the gospel. There's the gospel, and there's rap in step with the gospel. There's the gospel, and there's music in step with the gospel. There's the gospel, and there's advertising for your music group in step with the gospel. There's the gospel, and there's the clothing and the attitudes and the tone that's in step with the gospel. So it's a more biblical test for a rap song to ask, "Is the message, the tone, the aim, the origin, the spirit, in step with the gospel, rather than does it contain the four or five or six points or steps of the gospel?" If you tried to put a summary of the gospel in every song, it would start to sound stilted. It would be like saying, "Every chapter in the New Testament has to begin the way 1 Corinthians 15 begins. Now I would remind you, brothers, of the gospel, that Christ died for our sins in accordance with the scriptures, that he was buried, that he was raised on the third day in accordance with the scriptures."
