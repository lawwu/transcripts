The first caution is, I think it is invalid to persuade yourself to leave a job and enter a new ministry if the argument that you use to persuade yourself necessarily puts other obedient people in the category of disobedient. Let me try to explain. Joshua says, "I began to feel as though a life spent in the workforce or corporate America was a waste of time and energy." So my counter question is, does Joshua believe that all other Christians who spend their life in the workforce or corporate America, or who spend their career in tasks that are not the direct mission of spreading the gospel, are those people living in disobedience?

One, no Christian should work in the ordinary marketplace, or two, the argument that Joshua is using to leave is invalid. As I read the New Testament, it is clear that the apostles do not intend that all Christians should leave their ordinary employment.

The principle that Paul follows in 1 Corinthians 7.20 is each one should remain in the condition in which he was called, that he's called to be a Christian. Or, verse 24, "So, brothers, in whatever condition each of you was called, let him remain with God." Now, "with God" changes everything.

It's not as though the apostles don't want there to be radical change in your life and in the way you relate to people and your job, but you don't have to leave your job in order to be a faithful Christian according to those principles. So that's my first caution.

If, Joshua, you are going to leave your job to be sure that the reason you are leaving it does not put other people who don't leave in the position of being disobedient, because that would be an invalid reason, since God does not intend for everybody to leave. Second caution.

So be careful that you don't assume that a change of location or a change of vocation will mean that you are more devoted to Christ or more effective in winning lost people and more fruitful in glorifying the king. That's caution number two. Here's a third caution. Keep in mind that aiming to be a martyr is turning a God-decided result of faithfulness into a self-decided definition of faithfulness.

You shouldn't do that. It may be that the path of obedience that God assigns to you will end in martyrdom. That's not your call. God's calling on our life is obedience and faithfulness and love and zeal. How it ends is his business, not ours. And remember, many have been martyred who were weak and worldly Christians, who happened to be in the wrong place at the wrong time.

And many have died natural deaths who were vastly more fruitful and effective Christians than some martyrs. There is no necessary correlation between the fruitfulness of your life and whether someone kills you or not. Now, those are my three cautions. Here are my two exhortations. It is certainly true that God has mysteriously, wonderfully led thousands of people to leave ordinary jobs in their homeland in order to move them onto frontline missionary work.

That restlessness is not the only means God uses to lead people, but it is often part of it.

It certainly was part of it for me when I left one calling for another. And this restlessness is not an indictment of the other people who don't feel it. So I'm not telling Joshua to be quiet and stay where he is. Maybe not. This may indeed be God's moving in his life, which leads then to the last exhortation.

That's the way it ought to happen ordinarily. Not an isolated person trying to figure out the emotional state of their own soul or God's call on their life, but a member of a body of Christ using his gifts in relationship to other people and then discovering from the fruitfulness of his labors what he's effective at and what he loves to do and what others give him affirmation in doing.

So my answer to Joshua's question is, whether you become a martyr or not will not be the evidence of whether you have spent your life fully devoted to Jesus or wasted it. That's not the test.
